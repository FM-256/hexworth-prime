üì° Wireless Networking Fundamentals

Wireless LANs (WLANs) use radio waves instead of cables to connect devices.

Key Concepts

  • SSID - Network name broadcast by AP
  • BSSID - AP's MAC address
  • BSS - Basic Service Set (single AP)
  • ESS - Extended Service Set (multiple APs)
  • Channel - Specific frequency slice

How It Works

  • AP broadcasts beacon frames with SSID
  • Client discovers and associates with AP
  • Authentication occurs (open or secured)
  • Data frames transmitted over radio
  • CSMA/CA prevents collisions
üí° CSMA/CA vs CSMA/CD: Wireless uses Collision Avoidance (CA) because devices can't detect collisions while transmitting. Wired Ethernet uses Collision Detection (CD).

The 802.11 family defines wireless LAN specifications. Each standard improved speed, range, or features.

üìª Legacy Standards: 802.11a & 802.11b

The first widely deployed wireless standards, released the same year but very different.

802.11a (Wi-Fi 2)

Band: 5 GHz

Speed: Up to 54 Mbps

Range: Shorter (5 GHz doesn't penetrate walls well)

Channels: 23 non-overlapping

Modulation: OFDM

802.11b (Wi-Fi 1)

Band: 2.4 GHz

Speed: Up to 11 Mbps

Range: Longer (2.4 GHz penetrates walls better)

Channels: 3 non-overlapping (1, 6, 11)

Modulation: DSSS

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Both 802.11a and 802.11b are obsolete and rarely used today, but they're still on the exam! Remember: "a" = 5 GHz, "b" = 2.4 GHz.

üöÄ 802.11g & 802.11n (Wi-Fi 3 & 4)

These standards brought significant improvements in speed and introduced dual-band support.

802.11g (Wi-Fi 3) - 2003

Band: 2.4 GHz

Speed: Up to 54 Mbps

Key Feature: Backward compatible with 802.11b

Modulation: OFDM (like 802.11a)

802.11n (Wi-Fi 4) - 2009

Band: 2.4 GHz AND 5 GHz

Speed: Up to 600 Mbps

Key Features:

  • MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output)
  • Channel bonding (40 MHz channels)
  • Frame aggregation

üîë MIMO Explained

MIMO uses multiple antennas to send and receive multiple data streams simultaneously. A 4x4 MIMO system has 4 transmit and 4 receive antennas, dramatically increasing throughput.

‚ö° 802.11ac - Wi-Fi 5

Released in 2013, 802.11ac brought gigabit wireless speeds to the 5 GHz band.

Key Features

  • 5 GHz Only - Avoids crowded 2.4 GHz
  • Wider Channels - Up to 160 MHz
  • More MIMO Streams - Up to 8x8
  • MU-MIMO - Multi-User MIMO (Wave 2)
  • 256-QAM - More bits per symbol
  • Beamforming - Focused signal to clients
  • Max Speed - 6.9 Gbps theoretical
  • Typical Speed - 1-2 Gbps real-world

üìä Channel Widths

Wider channels = more speed but fewer available channels

  • 20 MHz: Standard width, most channels available
  • 40 MHz: 2x throughput, half the channels
  • 80 MHz: 4x throughput, limited channels
  • 160 MHz: 8x throughput, very few channels (use carefully!)
üìù Exam Tip: 802.11ac is 5 GHz ONLY! If you see "ac" and "2.4 GHz" together, it's wrong.

üåü 802.11ax - Wi-Fi 6 & Wi-Fi 6E

The latest standard, designed for high-density environments with many devices.

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax)

Bands: 2.4 GHz + 5 GHz

Speed: Up to 9.6 Gbps

Key Innovation: OFDMA

Wi-Fi 6E

Bands: 2.4 + 5 + 6 GHz

6 GHz Benefit: 1200 MHz of new spectrum!

Channels: 59 new 20 MHz channels

Wi-Fi 6 Key Technologies

  • OFDMA - Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access: Allows AP to communicate with multiple clients simultaneously on different subcarriers
  • 1024-QAM - 25% more data per symbol than Wi-Fi 5's 256-QAM
  • TWT - Target Wake Time: Clients schedule when to wake, saving battery
  • BSS Coloring - Reduces interference from neighboring networks
  • Uplink MU-MIMO - Multiple clients can transmit simultaneously
üéØ Why Wi-Fi 6E Matters: The 6 GHz band is "greenfield" - no legacy devices, no interference from old equipment. Perfect for high-performance applications.

üìä 5 GHz Channel Planning

The 5 GHz band offers many more non-overlapping channels but with some restrictions.

5 GHz Channel Groups (UNII Bands)

  • UNII-1 (36-48): Indoor use, low power
  • UNII-2A (52-64): Requires DFS (radar detection)
  • UNII-2C (100-144): Requires DFS, more channels
  • UNII-3 (149-165): Higher power allowed, no DFS

üõ°Ô∏è DFS - Dynamic Frequency Selection

Some 5 GHz channels are shared with radar systems. DFS requires the AP to:

  • Listen for radar signals before transmitting
  • Vacate the channel immediately if radar is detected
  • Wait 30 minutes before returning to that channel

Impact: DFS channels may cause brief disconnections when radar is detected.

‚úÖ Advantages

  • 24+ non-overlapping 20 MHz channels
  • Less interference than 2.4 GHz
  • Higher speeds with wider channels

‚ö†Ô∏è Considerations

  • Shorter range than 2.4 GHz
  • DFS channels may have interruptions
  • Some older devices don't support 5 GHz

‚ö†Ô∏è WEP and WPA (Legacy Security)

Understanding these protocols helps you recognize insecure configurations.

WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)

Released: 1999

Encryption: RC4 stream cipher

Problem: IV (Initialization Vector) is only 24 bits

Why It's Broken:

  • IVs repeat frequently on busy networks
  • Static key shared by all users
  • No protection against replay attacks
  • Can be cracked in under 5 minutes

WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access)

Released: 2003

Encryption: TKIP (Temporal Key Integrity Protocol)

Improvement: Dynamic keys, larger IV

Limitations:

  • Still uses RC4 (same as WEP)
  • TKIP has known vulnerabilities
  • Was a temporary fix until WPA2
  • Should not be used today
üìù Exam Tip: WEP = completely broken, never use. WPA = better than WEP but deprecated. Always choose WPA2 or WPA3.

üîí WPA2 (Wi-Fi Protected Access 2)

WPA2 has been the security standard since 2004 and is still widely used today.

Key Features

  • AES-CCMP: Strong AES encryption replaces weak RC4
  • CCMP: Counter Mode with CBC-MAC Protocol for integrity
  • Mandatory for Wi-Fi certification since 2006

WPA2-Personal (PSK)

Authentication: Pre-Shared Key

Use Case: Home, small office

How It Works:

  • All users share same passphrase
  • 4-way handshake derives session keys
  • Simple to deploy

WPA2-Enterprise (802.1X)

Authentication: RADIUS server

Use Case: Corporations, universities

How It Works:

  • Each user has unique credentials
  • EAP methods: PEAP, EAP-TLS
  • Centralized user management
üîë PSK Weakness: WPA2-Personal is vulnerable to offline dictionary attacks if the passphrase is weak. Use 12+ character random passphrases!

üõ°Ô∏è WPA3 - The Latest Standard

Released in 2018, WPA3 addresses weaknesses in WPA2 and adds new protections.

WPA3 Improvements

SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals)

Replaces 4-way handshake with "Dragonfly" key exchange

Benefit: Resistant to offline dictionary attacks

Forward Secrecy

Each session uses unique encryption keys

Benefit: Captured traffic can't be decrypted later

Protected Management Frames

PMF is mandatory (optional in WPA2)

Benefit: Prevents deauthentication attacks

192-bit Enterprise Mode

Stronger encryption for sensitive environments

Benefit: Government/financial grade security

üéØ Wi-Fi Easy Connect: WPA3 also introduces Easy Connect (DPP) for IoT devices - scan a QR code to securely onboard devices without typing passwords.

üîë Enterprise Authentication (802.1X)

Enterprise wireless uses 802.1X with EAP for individual user authentication.

802.1X Components

  • Supplicant: The client device requesting access
  • Authenticator: The access point (passes auth to server)
  • Authentication Server: RADIUS server that verifies credentials

Common EAP Methods

Method Credentials Security Complexity
EAP-TLS Client certificates üü¢ Highest High (PKI required)
PEAP Username/password üü¢ High Medium
EAP-TTLS Username/password üü¢ High Medium
EAP-FAST PAC or password üü° Good Low
üìù Exam Tip: EAP-TLS uses client certificates and is the most secure. PEAP wraps credentials in a TLS tunnel and is most common in enterprises.

üì° Access Point Types & Deployment

Different AP architectures suit different network sizes and management needs.

Autonomous (Standalone) APs

Management: Each AP configured individually

Best For: Small deployments (1-3 APs)

Pros: Simple, no controller needed

Cons: Doesn't scale, inconsistent config

Controller-Based (Lightweight) APs

Management: Centralized WLC

Best For: Enterprise (10+ APs)

Pros: Consistent config, easy roaming

Cons: Controller is single point of failure

Cloud-Managed APs

Management: Cloud dashboard

Best For: Distributed sites

Pros: No on-prem controller, remote management

Cons: Requires internet, subscription cost

CAPWAP Protocol

Controller-based APs use CAPWAP (Control and Provisioning of Wireless Access Points) to communicate with the WLC:

  • Control Channel (UDP 5246): Management traffic, encrypted
  • Data Channel (UDP 5247): User traffic, can be local or tunneled

üó∫Ô∏è Wireless Site Surveys

A site survey ensures proper AP placement and channel planning before deployment.

Types of Site Surveys

  • Predictive: Software simulation using floor plans
  • Passive: Walk around listening to existing signals
  • Active: Connect and measure actual performance
  • Post-deployment: Verify coverage after install

What to Measure

  • Signal Strength (RSSI): Target -65 dBm or better
  • Signal-to-Noise (SNR): Target 25 dB or better
  • Channel Utilization: Under 50% ideal
  • Interference Sources: Identify and mitigate

üìä Heat Maps

Site survey tools generate heat maps showing signal coverage:

  • üü¢ Green: Strong signal (-30 to -65 dBm)
  • üü° Yellow: Moderate signal (-65 to -75 dBm)
  • üî¥ Red: Weak signal (-75 to -85 dBm)
  • ‚ö´ Gray: No coverage (below -85 dBm)
üìù Exam Tip: Know that -30 dBm is STRONGER than -80 dBm. The closer to 0, the better the signal!

üîß Wireless Troubleshooting

Common wireless issues and how to diagnose them.

Slow Performance

Causes:

  • Channel interference/overlap
  • Too many clients on one AP
  • Distance from AP
  • Legacy clients forcing slower speeds

Fix: Check channel, add APs, upgrade clients

Intermittent Connectivity

Causes:

  • Interference (microwaves, Bluetooth)
  • DFS channel changes (5 GHz)
  • Roaming issues between APs
  • Power saving aggressive

Fix: Change channel, check for interference sources

Can't Connect

Causes:

  • Wrong password
  • Security mismatch (WPA2 vs WPA3)
  • MAC filtering blocking
  • AP at capacity

Fix: Verify credentials, check security settings

Coverage Holes

Causes:

  • Physical obstacles (concrete, metal)
  • Insufficient AP density
  • AP power too low
  • Wrong antenna type

Fix: Site survey, add APs, adjust power

üõ†Ô∏è Troubleshooting Tools: Wi-Fi analyzer apps, spectrum analyzers, ping/traceroute, AP logs, controller dashboards

üì± Other Wireless Technologies

Beyond Wi-Fi, several other wireless technologies are important for networking.

Bluetooth

Range: ~10 meters (Class 2)

Band: 2.4 GHz

Use: Headphones, keyboards, IoT

Versions: 4.0 (BLE), 5.0 (longer range)

Zigbee

Range: 10-100 meters

Band: 2.4 GHz

Use: Smart home, sensors

Feature: Mesh networking, low power

Z-Wave

Range: ~30 meters

Band: 908 MHz (US)

Use: Home automation

Feature: Less interference than 2.4 GHz

NFC

Range: ~4 cm

Band: 13.56 MHz

Use: Payments, access cards

Feature: Very short range = security

Cellular (4G/5G)

Range: Kilometers

Band: Various licensed bands

Use: Mobile data, WAN backup

5G: mmWave for high speed, sub-6 for coverage

Infrared (IR)

Range: Line of sight

Band: Infrared light

Use: Remote controls

Limitation: Requires direct line of sight
