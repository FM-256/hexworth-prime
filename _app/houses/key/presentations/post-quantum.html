Post-Quantum Cryptography

The quantum computing revolution is approaching. When large-scale quantum computers arrive, they will break most of the public-key cryptography that secures the internet today. Learn how the cryptographic community is preparing for this paradigm shift.

1. The Quantum Threat

Why Current Cryptography is Vulnerable

Modern public-key cryptography relies on mathematical problems that are hard for classical computers to solve, such as integer factorization (RSA) and discrete logarithms (ECC, Diffie-Hellman). Quantum computers, however, can solve these problems efficiently using specialized algorithms.

Critical Vulnerability

A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm can break RSA, ECC, and Diffie-Hellman in polynomial time, rendering most internet security protocols vulnerable. This includes TLS/SSL, VPNs, digital signatures, and cryptocurrency wallets.

Shor's Algorithm: Breaking Public-Key Cryptography

Developed by Peter Shor in 1994, this quantum algorithm can factor large numbers and compute discrete logarithms exponentially faster than the best known classical algorithms.

Mitigation Strategy

Double the key size: Use AES-256 instead of AES-128, and SHA-384/512 instead of SHA-256. Symmetric cryptography remains relatively safe with appropriate key lengths.

2. When Will Quantum Computers Arrive?

Predicting when cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) will exist is challenging. Estimates vary, but most experts agree it's a question of "when," not "if."

2019
Google achieves "quantum supremacy" with 53-qubit Sycamore processor, performing a specific task faster than classical supercomputers.
2023
IBM unveils 433-qubit Osprey processor. Industry focus shifts to error correction and practical quantum advantage.
2024
NIST finalizes first three post-quantum cryptographic standards (FIPS 203, 204, 205), marking the beginning of the PQC transition era.
2030s (Estimate)
Cryptographically relevant quantum computers may emerge. Estimates suggest ~4,000 logical qubits needed to break RSA-2048.
2035-2040 (Conservative)
More conservative estimates place CRQC development in this timeframe, accounting for error correction challenges.

The Problem: We Don't Know Exactly When

Breakthrough discoveries in quantum computing or error correction could dramatically accelerate the timeline. Nation-state actors may also have classified programs ahead of public knowledge. The prudent approach is to transition now.

3. NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization

The Competition

In 2016, NIST launched a public competition to identify quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms. After multiple rounds of evaluation, cryptanalysis, and community review, NIST selected the first winners in 2022 and published final standards in August 2024.

Standardization Complete (Primary Algorithms)

As of August 2024, NIST has published three Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS) for post-quantum cryptography, marking a historic milestone in cryptographic transition.

Selection Criteria

NIST evaluated candidate algorithms based on multiple factors:

  • Security: Resistance to both classical and quantum attacks
  • Performance: Speed of key generation, encryption, and decryption
  • Key/Ciphertext Sizes: Bandwidth and storage requirements
  • Simplicity: Ease of implementation and analysis
  • Versatility: Suitability for various use cases and constraints
  • Diversity: Based on different mathematical problems for redundancy

Why Multiple Winners?

NIST selected multiple algorithms to provide options for different use cases and to hedge against future cryptanalytic breakthroughs. If one mathematical approach is compromised, alternatives remain secure.

4. Lattice-Based Cryptography

The Core Concept

Lattice-based cryptography is the foundation of both NIST's primary winners: CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium. These schemes rely on the hardness of certain lattice problems, which remain difficult even for quantum computers.

Lattice Structure (Simplified 2D Representation)

A lattice is a regular arrangement of points in n-dimensional space. Finding the shortest vector or the closest vector to a target point is computationally hard.

Learning With Errors (LWE)

The security of lattice-based schemes relies on the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem, introduced by Oded Regev in 2005.

Given: A · s + e = b (mod q) where: A = random matrix s = secret vector e = small error vector b = result vector q = modulus Problem: Find s, given A and b

The "errors" make it hard to solve the system of linear equations. Even with many samples, recovering the secret vector s remains computationally infeasible, even for quantum computers (as far as we know).

  • Classical Hardness: Best known attacks take exponential time
  • Quantum Hardness: No known quantum algorithm provides significant speedup
  • Provable Security: LWE can be reduced to worst-case lattice problems
  • Versatility: Enables encryption, signatures, and advanced protocols

CRYSTALS-Kyber (ML-KEM) - Key Encapsulation

Module-Lattice Key Encapsulation Mechanism (ML-KEM), formerly known as CRYSTALS-Kyber, is NIST's primary choice for establishing shared secrets in post-quantum TLS and other key exchange protocols.

CRYSTALS-Dilithium (ML-DSA) - Digital Signatures

Module-Lattice Digital Signature Algorithm (ML-DSA), formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium, is NIST's primary recommendation for post-quantum digital signatures.

Parameter Set Security Level Public Key Signature Size
ML-DSA-44 ~AES-128 1,312 bytes 2,420 bytes
ML-DSA-65 ~AES-192 1,952 bytes 3,293 bytes
ML-DSA-87 ~AES-256 2,592 bytes 4,595 bytes

Size Trade-offs

Dilithium signatures are significantly larger than RSA or ECDSA signatures. An ECDSA signature is ~64 bytes, while ML-DSA-65 produces ~3,293-byte signatures. This impacts bandwidth, especially for constrained devices or high-volume signing operations.

Use Cases: Software updates, code signing, certificate authorities, document signing, blockchain transactions (post-quantum).

5. Hash-Based Signatures: SPHINCS+

Conservative Security Choice

SPHINCS+ (SLH-DSA) is NIST's backup signature standard, offering a fundamentally different approach based only on the security of hash functions. If lattice-based cryptography is ever broken, SPHINCS+ provides a safety net.

Stateless Hash-Based Signatures

Unlike earlier hash-based signature schemes (XMSS, LMS), SPHINCS+ is stateless, meaning it doesn't require tracking which signatures have been generated. This makes it much more practical for general use.

How SPHINCS+ Works

SPHINCS+ builds a signature scheme using only cryptographic hash functions through a complex tree structure. The security relies solely on the hash function's collision resistance and preimage resistance.

SPHINCS+ Tree Structure (Simplified)

Multiple layers of hash trees combine to create a signature. Each layer provides additional security and enables stateless operation.

Parameter Set Security Level Public Key Signature Size
SLH-DSA-128s ~AES-128 32 bytes 7,856 bytes
SLH-DSA-128f ~AES-128 32 bytes 17,088 bytes
SLH-DSA-256s ~AES-256 64 bytes 29,792 bytes

Performance Trade-offs

  • Pros: Very small public keys, conservative security assumptions
  • Cons: Large signatures (7KB-30KB), slower signing/verification
  • 's' variants: Smaller signatures, slower performance
  • 'f' variants: Larger signatures, faster performance

Best Use Cases: Long-term security, code signing, firmware updates, scenarios where bandwidth is less critical than security confidence.

6. Code-Based Cryptography: Classic McEliece

The Oldest Post-Quantum Candidate

Classic McEliece is based on the McEliece cryptosystem from 1978, making it one of the oldest and most studied post-quantum algorithms. It relies on the hardness of decoding random linear codes.

Time-Tested Security

After 45+ years of cryptanalysis, no significant attacks against properly implemented McEliece have been found. This long track record provides high confidence in its security.

The Size Problem

While Classic McEliece offers excellent security and performance, it has one major drawback: extremely large public keys.

Use Case Limitations

The large public key size makes Classic McEliece impractical for many applications, especially TLS certificates and constrained devices. However, it remains under consideration as a backup KEM due to its different mathematical foundation.

Best Use Cases: High-security government applications, long-term data protection, scenarios where key storage isn't constrained.

8. Migration Strategies and Crypto Agility

Cryptographic Inventory Assessment

Before migrating, organizations must understand their current cryptographic landscape.

Critical First Step: Discovery

Many organizations don't have complete visibility into where cryptography is used:

  • TLS/SSL certificates and connections
  • Code signing and software updates
  • VPN and remote access systems
  • Database encryption and key management
  • IoT device authentication
  • Backup and archive encryption
  • Embedded cryptography in third-party software
Inventory Checklist: ✓ Identify all systems using RSA, ECC, DH ✓ Document key sizes and lifetimes ✓ Map data sensitivity and retention periods ✓ Identify legacy systems that cannot be updated ✓ Catalog third-party dependencies ✓ Assess performance and bandwidth constraints

Crypto Agility

Crypto agility is the ability to quickly swap cryptographic algorithms without major system redesign. This is essential for PQC migration and future security.

Crypto Agile Architecture
Application Layer
Business logic (algorithm-agnostic)
↕

Design Principles for Agility

  • Use standard cryptographic APIs (don't hard-code algorithms)
  • Store algorithm identifiers with encrypted data
  • Support algorithm negotiation in protocols
  • Separate keys from algorithms (key derivation independence)
  • Plan for hybrid modes during transitions
  • Automate testing across algorithm combinations

Phased Migration Roadmap

Phase Timeline Actions Priority Systems
1. Assessment 2024-2025 Inventory, risk assessment, planning All systems
2. Preparation 2025-2026 Update libraries, test PQC, build agility Core infrastructure
3. Hybrid Deployment 2026-2028 Deploy hybrid modes, monitor performance Public-facing services
4. PQC-Only 2028-2035 Phase out classical algorithms Based on quantum threat

High-Priority Systems (Migrate First)

  • Long-term data storage (HNDL threat)
  • Root CA certificates (5-20 year lifetimes)
  • Government/classified communications
  • Healthcare and financial records
  • Intellectual property and trade secrets

Challenges and Considerations

  • Performance Impact: PQC algorithms are generally slower and produce larger keys/signatures. Test thoroughly in production-like environments.
  • Bandwidth Constraints: Large signatures may impact protocols with size limits (DNS, IoT, embedded systems).
  • Legacy System Compatibility: Some systems cannot be updated. Plan for gateways, proxies, or isolation strategies.
  • Supply Chain Dependencies: Hardware, firmware, and third-party software must all support PQC. Engage vendors early.
  • Regulatory Compliance: Government agencies may mandate PQC migration timelines. Stay informed of requirements (e.g., NIST, CISA, NSA guidance).
  • Training and Expertise: Staff must understand new algorithms, implementation risks, and testing procedures.

9. Real-World Adoption and Experiments

Industry Leaders Pioneering PQC

Google & Cloudflare

In 2023, announced support for X25519Kyber768 hybrid key exchange in Chrome and Cloudflare's infrastructure. Protects billions of TLS connections against future quantum threats.

Signal Messenger

Implemented PQXDH (Post-Quantum Extended Diffie-Hellman) combining X25519 with Kyber for end-to-end encrypted messaging. Deployed to millions of users.

Apple iMessage PQ3

Announced in 2024, PQ3 protocol uses post-quantum cryptography with continuous key rotation, claiming "level 3" post-quantum security for iMessage.

AWS & Microsoft

Cloud providers offering PQC options in TLS libraries (s2n-TLS), Azure VPN, and cryptographic services. Enabling customer adoption at scale.

NSA & CISA

U.S. government agencies issuing guidance for federal systems to transition to PQC. NSA's Commercial National Security Algorithm Suite 2.0 specifies timelines.

Open Quantum Safe

Open-source project (liboqs) providing PQC implementations for experimentation. Integrated into OpenSSL forks, allowing developers to test PQC today.

Government Mandates and Timelines

U.S. Government Requirements

The National Security Memorandum (NSM-10) directs federal agencies to transition to post-quantum cryptography. Key dates and requirements:

  • 2024-2025: Complete cryptographic inventory
  • 2025-2030: Migrate National Security Systems (NSS)
  • 2030-2035: Full transition for all federal systems
  • Contractors: Must demonstrate PQC readiness for classified work

International Efforts: EU's ENISA, UK's NCSC, and other national cyber agencies are issuing similar guidance. Global coordination is critical for internet-wide cryptographic transitions.
