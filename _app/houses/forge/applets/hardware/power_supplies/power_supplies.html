‚ö° What Does a PSU Do?

The Power Supply Unit (PSU) converts AC power from the wall outlet to DC power that computer components require. It also regulates voltage to provide stable, clean power.

AC to DC Conversion

Input (AC)
110-120V (US) or 220-240V (EU)
From wall outlet
Output (DC)
+3.3V, +5V, +12V, -12V
For components
üí° A+ Exam Tip: The +12V rail is most important for modern systems - it powers the CPU and GPU. Most components now use +12V rather than +5V or +3.3V.

üìä 80 PLUS Efficiency Ratings

The 80 PLUS certification indicates a PSU operates at a minimum of 80% efficiency, meaning at least 80% of AC power is converted to DC (less wasted as heat).

80 PLUS Standard

80%

80 PLUS Bronze

82%

80 PLUS Silver

85%

80 PLUS Gold

87%

80 PLUS Platinum

90%

80 PLUS Titanium

94%
üí° Why Efficiency Matters: A 500W system with 80% efficiency draws 625W from the wall. At 90% efficiency, it draws only 556W - saving electricity and reducing heat.

üßÆ Calculating Power Requirements

Calculate total wattage needed by adding component power draws, then add 20-30% headroom.

Typical Component Power Draw

Component Typical Wattage
CPU (mid-range) 65-125W
CPU (high-end) 125-250W
GPU (mid-range) 150-200W
GPU (high-end) 250-450W
RAM (per stick) 3-5W
SSD 2-5W
HDD 5-10W
Motherboard 25-80W
üìù Example: CPU (125W) + GPU (300W) + Motherboard (50W) + RAM (10W) + Storage (10W) = 495W base. Add 30% headroom = 650W PSU recommended.
