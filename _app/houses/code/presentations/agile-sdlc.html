Master the development methodologies that guide software creation from concept to deployment. Understanding both traditional and Agile approaches is essential for modern DevOps practitioners.

What is the Software Development Life Cycle?

The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a structured process for planning, creating, testing, and deploying software systems. It provides a framework for delivering high-quality software that meets customer requirements and is completed within time and cost estimates.

1
Planning
Define project scope, requirements, and feasibility
2
Analysis
Gather detailed requirements and define system specifications
3
Design
Create architecture, UI/UX, and database designs
4
Development
Write code and build the actual software
5
Testing
Verify quality, find bugs, ensure requirements are met
6
Deployment
Release software to production environment
7
Maintenance
Monitor, update, and fix issues post-deployment

Traditional SDLC Models

üíß Waterfall Model
‚ñº

A linear, sequential approach where each phase must be completed before the next begins. Like a waterfall, progress flows in one direction.

  • Advantages: Simple, easy to understand and manage; well-documented; good for stable requirements
  • Disadvantages: Inflexible to changes; testing happens late; customer sees product only at the end
  • Best for: Projects with well-defined, unchanging requirements (e.g., government contracts, embedded systems)
‚úì V-Model (Validation & Verification)
‚ñº

Extension of Waterfall where each development phase has a corresponding testing phase. Forms a "V" shape when visualized.

  • Advantages: Testing is planned early; defects found earlier; clear milestones
  • Disadvantages: Still rigid; expensive to make changes; no working software until late
  • Best for: Safety-critical systems (medical devices, aerospace) where testing rigor is paramount
üîÑ Spiral Model
‚ñº

Combines iterative development with systematic aspects of Waterfall. Emphasizes risk analysis through repeated cycles (spirals).

  • Advantages: Risk management built-in; flexible to changes; early customer feedback
  • Disadvantages: Complex; expensive; requires risk assessment expertise
  • Best for: Large, complex, high-risk projects (enterprise systems, R&D projects)

The Agile Manifesto

We have come to value:

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
Working software over comprehensive documentation
Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

Core Agile Principles

üöÄ Iterative & Incremental Development
‚ñº

Deliver working software frequently in short iterations (1-4 weeks). Each iteration produces a potentially shippable product increment.

  • Faster time to market with MVP (Minimum Viable Product)
  • Early and continuous delivery of valuable software
  • Ability to adapt to changing requirements even late in development
üë• Customer Collaboration
‚ñº

Business stakeholders and developers work together daily throughout the project. Customer feedback drives priorities.

  • Regular demos and reviews with stakeholders
  • Product Owner represents customer needs
  • Welcome changing requirements as competitive advantage
üéØ Self-Organizing Teams
‚ñº

The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams. Trust the team to figure out how to accomplish the work.

  • Cross-functional teams with all skills needed
  • Team decides how to accomplish sprint goals
  • Continuous improvement through retrospectives
üìä Sustainable Pace
‚ñº

Agile processes promote sustainable development. Sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.

  • No death marches or excessive overtime
  • Realistic sprint commitments based on velocity
  • Focus on long-term productivity over short-term bursts

Scrum Framework

üë• Scrum Roles

  • Product Owner: Defines features, prioritizes backlog, accepts/rejects work
  • Scrum Master: Facilitates process, removes impediments, coaches team
  • Development Team: Self-organizing, cross-functional, delivers increments

üìÖ Scrum Ceremonies

  • Sprint Planning: Define sprint goal and select backlog items
  • Daily Standup: 15-min sync (what did, what will, blockers)
  • Sprint Review: Demo completed work to stakeholders
  • Sprint Retrospective: Reflect and improve processes

üìã Scrum Artifacts

  • Product Backlog: Ordered list of all desired features
  • Sprint Backlog: Committed items for current sprint
  • Increment: Sum of all completed backlog items
  • Burndown Chart: Visual of remaining work vs time

Kanban Basics

Kanban is a visual workflow management method that helps teams visualize work, limit work-in-progress (WIP), and maximize efficiency. Unlike Scrum, Kanban has no fixed sprints - work flows continuously.

Core Kanban Principles:

  • Visualize Workflow: Make all work visible on a Kanban board (To Do ‚Üí In Progress ‚Üí Done)
  • Limit WIP: Set maximum items allowed in each column to prevent overload
  • Manage Flow: Optimize the speed and smoothness of work moving through the system
  • Make Policies Explicit: Clear rules for moving items between columns
  • Continuous Improvement: Regularly analyze metrics (cycle time, throughput) to improve

Agile vs Traditional Waterfall

Aspect Waterfall Agile
Approach Sequential, linear phases Iterative, incremental sprints
Requirements Fixed at start (detailed upfront) Evolving (welcome changes)
Customer Involvement Beginning and end only Continuous throughout
Testing After development phase Continuous in each iteration
Delivery Single delivery at the end Frequent releases (every sprint)
Flexibility Difficult and expensive to change Embraces change
Team Structure Specialized roles, hierarchical Cross-functional, self-organizing
Documentation Comprehensive, detailed Just enough, focus on working software
Best For Well-defined, stable projects Dynamic, evolving projects

How Agile Connects to DevOps

DevOps is the natural evolution of Agile principles applied to operations. While Agile broke down walls between business and development, DevOps breaks down walls between development and operations.

Choosing the Right Methodology

üö´ Choose Waterfall When:

  • Requirements are stable and well-understood
  • Technology is mature and familiar
  • Regulatory compliance requires extensive documentation
  • Fixed price, fixed scope contracts

‚úì Choose Agile When:

  • Requirements are expected to change
  • Customer wants to see early, frequent deliveries
  • Time to market is critical
  • Team can self-organize and collaborate

üîÑ Hybrid Approach:

  • Use Waterfall for infrastructure/architecture planning
  • Use Agile for feature development
  • Common in large enterprises transitioning to Agile
  • Example: "Water-Scrum-Fall"
